What Is
Reality?

An inquiry into the nature of reality

01

Introduction

For thousands of years, philosophers have grappled with some of the simplest yet most profound questions about human existence. These questions can range from what is the meaning of life and what is time. Through reasoning and debate, philosophers developed theories that continue to shape our understanding of reality today. However, as society undergoes rapid advancements in technology and artificial intelligence, these theories should be reconsidered and interpreted within a modern context. This website aims to explore the philosophical question "Is reality physical, mental, or both?" by examining key metaphysical and Catholicism perpectives as well as The Truman Show.

02

Two Metaphysics Theories

01
Property Dualism

According to EBSCO, property dualism is a philosophical theory which argues that the body, including the brain, is physical, but the mind is composed of mental properties. Property dualists believe that although the brain consists of physical matter, it can produce both physical properties like neurons and mental properties like consciousness.

This perspective views the brain as physical substance possessing two different types of properties: physical and mental. The physical properties include biological and chemical processes in the brain, while the mental properties include subjective experiences like thoughts and emotions. Due to its complexity, property dualists argue that the brain is capable of creating mental phenomena from physical processes.

In short, property dualism ultimately supports the idea that reality is both physical and mental. It explains that while everything may originate from physical matter such as the brain, its complex functioning produces mental properties including consciousness, thoughts, and emotions. However, these mental experiences cannot be fully reduced to physical processes alone, yet they clearly depend on the physical brain to exist.

Therefore, property dualism requires one to acknowledge both the physical structures of the world and the mental experiences that emerge from them, gaining a better understanding of what reality truly is. Furthermore, this perspective helps bridge the gap between science (studies the physical brain) and philosophy (explores the nature of the mind and consciousness).

Key philosophers: David Chalmers · Frank Jackson · Thomas Nagel
02
Materialism

According to Britannica, materialism is the philosophical theory that all facts (including those about the human mind, free will, and history) are ultimately dependent on physical processes. In other words, everything that exists can be explained through matter and physical interactions/ This idea can be traced back to early Greek philosophers more than 2000 years ago. They believed that the universe is composed entirely of material substance.

Materialism is based on two claims. First, material matter is the only substance that truly exists. Second, everything that appears mental like thoughts, emotions, and consciousness is the result of physical processes within matter. According to this perspective, mental or spiritual substance does not exist independently in the physical world.

Materialists simply view the human brain as a physical organ that functions through chemical reactions and electrical signals between neurons. These physical processes produce what we experience as mental states such as thinking, feeling emotions, and being conscious. However, materialists argue that these mental experiences are not independent entities. but are simply the result of brain activity.

For example, neuroscience shows that specific emotions and thoughts correspond to patterns of neural activity in the brain. When someone experiences happiness or fear, scientists can observe changes in brain chemistry and electrical signals.

Therefore, materialism argues that reality is entirely physical. According to this theory, consciousness, thoughts, and emotions are all reducible to physical processes occurring in the brain. What we call mental experiences are simply products of physical matter, meaning reality is purely physical as opposed to physical and mental.

Key philosophers: Thomas Hobbes · Patricia Churchland · Daniel Dennett

Catholicism Perspective

From Genesis 1:27, Imago Dei refers to the spiritual part of humanity. This theological concept has caused Catholics to view a human as a participant in two dimensions of reality: physical and mental/spiritual. Physical refers to the human body that exists in the physical world, while mental/spiritual refers to human intellect and will, reflecting God.

03 — The Truman Show (1998)

A World Built
for One Man

Directed by Peter Weir and starring Jim Carrey, The Truman Show (1998) tells the story of Truman Burbank, a man who unknowingly lives his entire life inside a massive television set. His family, friends, town, and daily experiences are all part of a reality TV show. So, everyone around him is an actor except for Truman himself.

"Good morning, and in case I don't see ya: good afternoon, good evening, and good night."

— Truman Burbank, The Truman Show (1998)

The film explores important philosophical questions about reality, perception, and free will. Truman's physical environment is real in the sense it exists, while also being artificially constructed. Therefore, his understanding of reality is based on how his mind perceives his surroundings.

🏙️
The Physical World

Seahaven was built from physical elements, making it real to Truman. He experiences physical sensations like warmth, cold, joy, fear, showcasing how his brain produces physical stimuli. The matter is real despite the set being constructed.

🧠
The Mental Construction

The world was designed, scripted, and maintained by humans. The film director, Christof established Truman's storyline, intentionally tailoring it to appease viewers.

🚪
The Moment of Freedom

When Truman exits the set by walks through the door at the edge of the dome, he chooses subjective truth over physical comfort, affirming that consciousness and inner experience cannot be fully reduced to any physical matter.

04 — Philosophical Analysis

Why The Truman Show
Proves Reality Is Both

The Truman Show is not merely a metaphor for reality; it is a philosophical argument. Analyzed through the lens of materialism and property Dualism, the film demonstrates why neither theory alone can account for the full nature of what is real. Reality, the film insists, is irreducibly both physical and mental.

Argument 01 — The Materialist Reading Is Incomplete

A materialist would argue that Truman's reality is simply whatever his brain processes as physical input. Since he genuinely receives physical stimuli like light, sound, touch, warmth, his reality is real by definition. The origin of those stimuli is irrelevant. This is a coherent position, but it leads to a volatile conclusion: any physical simulation is as real as "nature." The film challenges this idea by showcasing how Truman's discovery the truth alters his perception reality. The same physical world that felt real is suddenly not. This shows that physical objects alone cannot determine what is real to a conscious mind.

Argument 02 — Property Dualism Explains Truman's Inner Life

Property dualism highlights the film's deepest tension. Truman's brain is a physical object; neurons firing in response to physical stimuli. However his growing sense of unease, his love for Sylvia, his existential dread when he realizes the sun follows are all mental properties that have no correlation to his neural activity. Even if Christof could control every neuron in Truman's brain, he could not fully dictate what he experiences subjectively. The film makes this explicit; Christof only has control over Truman's physical world but not his inner thoughts. This is Property Dualism in action.

Argument 03 — The Act of Walking Through the Door

The film's climax is philosophically precise. When Truman reaches the edge of the dome and chooses to walk through the door, he makes a decision that cannot be fully explained in physical terms. No physical cause compels him to leave. In fact, every physical incentive (safety, familiarity, comfort) argues for staying. What drives him through is a mental conviction about authenticity, freedom, and the irreducibility of his own inner life. His choice affirms that consciousness can override physical determinism. The film's argument is that a reality is not composed soley of physical matter; mental properties are a part of it too.

Argument 04 — Imago Dei Analysis

Following Imago Dei, a Catholic would argue that Truman's ability to think critically, question his environment, and choose to leave the artificial world demonstrates his embodiment of mental and spiritual qualities within this theological concept.

✦

As a viewer, our instinct is that Truman deserves freedom is itself a metaphysical claim about the irreducibility of the inner life. If reality were purely physical, then a perfect simulation would be indistinguishable from "real" experience and Truman would have no legitimate complaint. The fact that we feel, viscerally, that he has been wronged, suggests that we already believe, intuitively, in the reality and importance of the mental.

05 — Conclusion

Reality Is Both

Having examined property dualism, materialism and the philosophical argument embedded in The Truman Show, we arrive at a position that neither pure Materialism nor any form of idealism can fully sustain: reality is both physical and mental.

The Physical Dimension

Matter is real. Truman's world has physical weight. Science explains a vast amount of what exists through physical laws. The brain is a physical organ, and mind depends on it. Materialism is not wrong; it is incomplete.

The Mental Dimension

Consciousness is real. The first-person experience of being alive — of seeing, feeling, choosing — cannot be fully captured by any physical description. Property Dualism honors this irreducible dimension of existence.

What Truman Shows Us

Truman's world is physically real and mentally constructed simultaneously. His liberation is a victory of consciousness over physical constraint. Reality, like Truman, cannot be reduced to either matter or mind alone.

The Philosophical Answer

The question "is reality physical, mental, or both?" may not have a final answer. But the evidence from philosophy and film converges on the same tentative conclusion: it is both — and the relationship between them remains the deepest mystery in philosophy.

✦

The most honest philosophical position is that we cannot step outside our own consciousness to verify what reality ultimately is. Like Truman, we can only press against the walls of our world and choose to walk through the door — trusting that what lies beyond is worth the uncertainty.
